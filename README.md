# LLM-usage-
The presentation is basically about the different ways you can use AI (LLMs specifically), going from the most basic to the most advanced.


It starts with prompting. just talking to ChatGPT or Claude in plain language, no code, nothing technical. Most people stop here.
Then there's vibe coding, where you describe what you want to build and the AI writes the code for you. The slides go pretty deep on this one, mentioning that Andrej Karpathy (ex-Tesla, OpenAI co-founder) coined the term in February 2025. There's even a real story about a team that vibe-coded an entire authentication system, everyone was happy at first, then requirements changed and the whole thing collapsed. The point being. Vibe coding is great for quick prototypes and personal scripts, but dangerous in production. The most valuable skill at that point becomes debugging, not coding.


After that it covers enterprise AI, connecting AI to actual business tools and workflows, think agents that handle emails, search internal documents, update databases automatically.


Then AI Engineer, a proper technical role focused on building products with LLM APIs. Not the same as machine learning, more about integration and building on top of existing models.


And finally Forward Deployed Engineer. Basically a hybrid between an engineer and a consultant, you go work on site with clients to make AI actually fit their specific needs. Palantir pioneered this model.


It wraps up with 5 prompting tips — be specific, use action verbs, break complex tasks into steps, give the AI a role, and always iterate.
